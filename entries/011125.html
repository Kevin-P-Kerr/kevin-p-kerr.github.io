Fermat's Factorization Method

Fermat tells us that, since every odd composite is the difference of two squares, we can factor any such number the following way. For example, `55=(8+3)(8-3)`


let y be the odd composite we wish to factor (assuming y is not a square). then find a square x such that `x+y=z`, where z is also square then let j,k be the roots of those squares respectively then `(k+j)(k-j)= k^2 - j^2 = z-x = y`, therefore `(k+j), (k-j)` are factors of y.


But how do we know that every off composite is the difference of two squares? Consider the following


If y is a non-square odd composite, then `j*k=y`, and j and k are odd. Let `j>k`. If j and k are odd, then `j+k` and `j-k` are even, and the midpoint between j and k, `(j+k)/2` is a whole number. Furthermore, the difference between the midpoint and either factor is `(j-k)/2`, which is also a whole number. Call the midpoint a, and the difference between the midpoint and the two factors b. Then `j=(a+b)` and `k=(a-b)`, `jk=(a+b)(a-b)=a^2-b^2=y`, as required.
