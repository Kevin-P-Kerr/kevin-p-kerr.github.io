Some Simple Theorems About Numbers

1. The Sum of Coprime Numbers is Coprime to Both

Let m,n be two coprime numbers, and let `q=m+n`
Suppose `q=p*m` for some p. Then `p*m=m+n` and `n=p*m-m=m(p-q)` which violates our hypothesis. And similar reasoning applies to n.

2. The Difference of Coprime Numbers is Coprime to Both

let m,n be the two numbers and in question, and let `p=n-n`
let `d=m-n`. Then `d>=1`. Suppose `d>1`, then hypothesis says that d is coprime to both m and n.
Suppose this is not so. Then `d=p*m` or `d=p*n` for some p. Say that `d=p*m`.
Then `m-n=p*m` and `p*m-m-n=0` and `p*m-m=n` and `m(p-1)=n.` which violates our hypothesis. and similar reasoning applies to m.

3. Adjacent Numbers Are Coprime

Let m,n be adjacent numbers, that is, `m-n=1`. Then m and n are coprime.
For suppose they are not. Then `m=p*n` and `p*n-n=1`.
Then `n(p-1)=1`, which has no integer solution, and is therefore absurd--i.e. violates our hypothesis.

4. Of Two Coprime Numbers, the Lesser can Be Added Either to 1 or to a Number Coprime to Both to Produce the Greater

Let m,n be the coprime numbers, with `m>n`. Then there is a p coprime to m and n such that `p+n=m`. If the two numbers are adjacent, the `p=1`, but say they are not adjacent.
Let `d=m-n`, then d is coprime to m and n by (2) above, and `d+n=m`
